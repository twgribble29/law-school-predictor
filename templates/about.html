About the Author

The story behind this project.

Ty Gribble

Data scientist and law school applicant who built this tool to bring transparency and data-driven insights to the admissions process.

Why I Built This

The law school admissions process is notoriously opaque. Applicants spend months agonizing over where to apply, often relying on anecdotal advice from forums or expensive admissions consultants. Meanwhile, a wealth of self-reported admissions data exists on platforms like LSD.Law—but it's difficult for most people to interpret.

I built this predictor to make that data actionable. By training machine learning models on over 100,000 historical application outcomes, this tool gives applicants a clear, statistical picture of their chances at any ABA-accredited law school.

Approach

This isn't a simple "median LSAT/GPA" lookup. The model accounts for temporal trends (acceptance rates have dropped 15–23 percentage points post-2020), interaction effects between your stats and school competitiveness, application timing, and—when available—your outcomes at other schools.

Every design decision was driven by rigorous analysis: we validated against held-out test data, checked calibration across deciles, analyzed errors by school tier and applicant profile, and implemented temporal weighting to ensure predictions reflect the current admissions landscape.

Limitations & Ethics

I want to be transparent about what this tool can't do:

  • It cannot capture holistic factors (personal statements, recommendations, diversity contributions)
  • The training data is self-reported and may contain biases
  • URM status is self-reported and imperfect
  • Past admissions patterns don't guarantee future outcomes

This tool is meant to inform your decisions, not make them for you. Use it as one data point among many.
